Family time

Teach Your Family About Pipeline Safety

Safety sticks when it's learned together. Turn it into a game with a pipeline scavenger hunt in and near your home.

The Pipeline Scavenger Hunt

Take a walk with your kids around your home and neighborhood and see how many of these you can find together:

  1. Your gas meter

    Usually beside the house or in an alley. Explain that the utility owns the pipes up to the meter — and your family owns the pipes from the meter to the stove, furnace and water heater.

  2. A pipeline marker

    Look for yellow or white posts where pipelines cross roads, railroads or fence lines. Read the marker together: what product does it carry, who operates it, and what's the emergency number?

  3. The shut-off valve

    Show older kids where your gas shut-off is located — knowledge for adults to use, not children.

  4. The "rotten egg" smell

    Explain mercaptan: a harmless odor added to natural gas so leaks are easy to notice. It smells like rotten eggs or a skunk. If they ever smell it, the rule is simple: tell an adult and get outside.

Make a simple family plan

  • Agree on a meeting spot a safe distance from the house, upwind if possible
  • Teach kids: if you smell rotten eggs or hear hissing — don't touch light switches or phones, just leave and tell an adult
  • Once everyone is safely away, an adult calls 911 and the gas company
  • Never go back inside until responders say it's safe

House rules near pipelines

No digging games, fort building or metal stakes near a pipeline right-of-way, and kites and drones stay clear of marked areas. If a ball rolls onto a fenced pipeline facility — leave it and tell an adult.

911

Suspect a pipeline leak?

Leave the area on foot immediately and warn others. From a safe distance, call 911 and the pipeline company on the marker. Don’t use phones, switches, vehicles or anything that could spark near the leak.
