What Do Pipelines Do?

Pipelines are the quiet infrastructure behind everyday Texas life — from the stove in your kitchen to the fuel in your truck.

Every day, millions of miles of pipeline across the United States safely move natural gas, crude oil, gasoline, diesel, jet fuel and other products. Texas is the heart of that network, with more than 480,000 miles of pipeline — far more than any other state. Pipelines heat homes, cook meals, fuel vehicles and supply the raw materials behind plastics, fertilizers, medicines and countless other products.

Three kinds of pipelines

  1. Gathering lines

    Smaller lines that collect natural gas or oil from wells and move it to processing facilities. Common in producing regions across rural Texas.

  2. Transmission lines

    The large, higher-pressure interstate and intrastate highways of the system, moving products long distances between regions, plants and cities. These are the lines you'll see marked along rights-of-way.

  3. Distribution lines

    The local network of smaller mains and service lines that deliver odorized natural gas to homes and businesses — right up to your meter.

Who's responsible for what

Pipeline operators own and maintain lines up to your gas meter. The piping from the meter to your appliances belongs to you, the property owner, and should be inspected periodically — some gas utilities offer this service, and licensed plumbers are authorized to do it too.

Why pipelines?

Pipelines are widely recognized as the safest, most efficient way to move energy products over land — far safer than moving the same volume by truck or rail. Learn more on Are Pipelines Safe?

911

Suspect a pipeline leak?

Leave the area on foot immediately and warn others. From a safe distance, call 911 and the pipeline company on the marker. Don’t use phones, switches, vehicles or anything that could spark near the leak.
