Pipeline safety

Signs of a Pipeline Leak

Use your eyes, ears and nose. Knowing how to recognize a leak — and what to do next — is the single most important pipeline safety skill.

Sight

  • Liquid pools, an oily sheen on water, or continuous bubbling in wet or flooded areas
  • Dead or discolored vegetation in an otherwise healthy area
  • Discolored, sunken or unusually dry soil near a pipeline right-of-way
  • Blowing dirt, a vapor cloud or mist, or frozen ground in warm weather
  • Fire coming from the ground or appearing to burn above ground

Sound

  • An unusual hissing, whistling or roaring sound near a pipeline — volume can range from quiet to loud depending on the size of the leak

Smell

  • A rotten-egg or skunk-like odor — natural gas in distribution lines is odorized with mercaptan so leaks are easier to notice
  • A petroleum or unusual chemical odor near a right-of-way
  • Note: gas in some transmission and gathering lines is not odorized, so never rely on smell alone

If you suspect a leak: do this

  • Leave the area immediately, on foot, moving upwind — and warn others to stay away
  • If you're indoors, get outside first
  • From a safe distance, call 911, then call the pipeline company listed on the nearest marker

Never do this near a suspected leak

  • Don't use phones, light switches, doorbells, garage openers or any electronic device
  • Don't start or drive vehicles or equipment, and don't drive into a vapor cloud
  • Don't light matches or create any flame or spark
  • Don't touch escaping liquids or gas, and don't try to operate pipeline valves
  • Don't attempt to put out a pipeline fire — wait for trained responders

In your home

If you have gas appliances and smell sulfur or rotten eggs, you may have a gas leak. Leave the house or building immediately — don't turn anything on or off on your way out. When you're a safe distance away, call 911 or your gas utility. The pipes that connect your appliances to the utility's line are owned and maintained by you, the property owner; have them inspected periodically by your gas company or a licensed plumber. If you have gas appliances, also consider installing a carbon monoxide detector.

If you hit a line while digging

Even if a line looks undamaged, call the pipeline company. A gouge, scrape or dent to a pipe or its coating can cause a leak or rupture later. Never cover up or bury damage.

911

Suspect a pipeline leak?

Leave the area on foot immediately and warn others. From a safe distance, call 911 and the pipeline company on the marker. Don’t use phones, switches, vehicles or anything that could spark near the leak.
