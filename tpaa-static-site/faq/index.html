Frequently Asked Questions

Straight answers to the questions Texans ask us most.

Is calling 811 really free?

Yes. Calling 811 (or submitting your project at texas811.org) is a free service. Utility operators mark their lines at no cost to you. Texas law requires you to make the request at least two working days before you dig.

Do I have to call 811 for a small project like planting a shrub?

Yes. Depth of buried lines varies and erosion can leave them shallower than you expect, so even small projects need a locate. If you hire a contractor, confirm they have called before work starts.

How deep are pipelines buried?

It varies by line type, age, terrain and erosion — which is why you can never assume a safe depth. Markers show approximate location only. Only an 811 locate tells you what is actually under your project.

What does a leak smell like?

Natural gas in distribution lines is odorized with mercaptan, which smells like rotten eggs or a skunk. Gas in some transmission and gathering lines is not odorized, so use your eyes and ears too — never rely on smell alone.

What should I do if I suspect a leak?

Leave the area immediately on foot, moving upwind, and warn others. From a safe distance, call 911 and the pipeline company listed on the nearest marker. Do not use phones, switches, vehicles or anything that could spark near the leak.

I hit a line while digging but it looks fine. Do I still need to report it?

Yes. Even a small gouge, scrape or dent to a pipe or its coating can cause a leak or rupture later. Call the pipeline company so they can inspect and repair it. Never cover up damage.

Who maintains the gas lines on my property?

The utility owns and maintains lines up to your meter. Piping from the meter to your appliances is owned by the property owner and should be inspected periodically — some gas companies offer this service, and licensed plumbers are authorized to do it.

There's a pipeline right-of-way on my property. What are my rights?

A copy of the legal right-of-way agreement is on file at your county courthouse. It defines what the operator may do in the easement and what activities are restricted. For questions or to report prohibited activity on a right-of-way, call the company listed on the pipeline marker.

How do I find pipelines near me?

Look for markers where lines cross roads and fence lines, and use the free public viewers from the National Pipeline Mapping System and the Railroad Commission of Texas. Before any digging, contact 811 — that is the only definitive answer.

How do I contact TPAA?

Email info@pipeline-safety.org with general questions or requests for information about the alliance. For emergencies, always call 911; for right-of-way issues, call the operator on the marker.
