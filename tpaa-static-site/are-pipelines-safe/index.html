Pipeline safety

Are Pipelines Safe?

You've seen the warning signs along the road. Here's how operators keep pipelines safe — and the part you play.

Pipelines are the safest way to transport energy products over land, and serious pipeline problems are rare — and in many cases preventable. Federal and state rules require operators to build, inspect, monitor and maintain their systems to strict standards, overseen by the U.S. Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA) and, in Texas, the Railroad Commission of Texas.

How operators keep pipelines safe

  • 24/7 monitoring. Control rooms watch pressure, flow and volume around the clock and can shut down a section of pipeline remotely if readings indicate a problem.
  • Regular inspection. Operators patrol rights-of-way by air and on the ground, run internal inspection tools ("smart pigs") through lines, and test for corrosion and coating damage.
  • Integrity management. Lines near populated and environmentally sensitive areas get extra assessment, testing and preventive maintenance on a set schedule.
  • Leak detection and odorization. Computerized leak-detection systems watch for anomalies, and distribution gas is odorized with mercaptan so people can smell a leak.
  • Trained, qualified people. Pipeline personnel are regularly trained and tested, and operators drill with local emergency responders.
  • Public awareness programs. Operators are required to educate the people who live and work near their lines — that's exactly what TPAA exists to do.

The biggest risk is accidental damage — and you can prevent it

The leading cause of serious pipeline incidents isn't equipment failure; it's third-party excavation damage. That's why the single most effective safety measure is also the simplest:

Call 811 before you dig

One free contact at least two working days before any digging project gets every buried line located and marked. Learn the four steps Texas law requires.

Your other role: be the operator's eyes

Report suspicious activity or unauthorized digging on a pipeline right-of-way to the company listed on the marker. And if you ever see, hear or smell the signs of a leak, leave the area and call 911 from a safe distance.

911

Suspect a pipeline leak?

Leave the area on foot immediately and warn others. From a safe distance, call 911 and the pipeline company on the marker. Don’t use phones, switches, vehicles or anything that could spark near the leak.
