Before you dig

Pipelines: Where Are They?

Texas has more pipeline mileage than any other state. Here's how to find out what runs near your home, land or job site.

Look for markers above ground

Yellow, white or orange pipeline markers show the approximate location of buried transmission pipelines. You'll usually find them where a pipeline crosses a street, highway, railroad or fence line. Each marker lists the product carried, the operator's name, and a 24-hour emergency phone number.

Markers are a clue, not a map

Markers don't show the exact route or depth of a line, and not every line is marked — distribution lines serving homes and businesses usually aren't. The only way to know exactly what's below your project is to contact 811 and have lines located for free.

Use free online map tools

  • National Pipeline Mapping System (NPMS) Run by the federal Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA). The public viewer shows transmission pipelines by county, with operator contact information.
  • Railroad Commission of Texas — Public GIS Viewer The RRC regulates intrastate pipelines in Texas and offers a public map viewer of pipelines across the state.
  • Texas811 Not a map, but the definitive answer: submit your dig site and every operator with lines in conflict will mark them at no cost to you.

Check your own property records

If a pipeline right-of-way crosses your property, a copy of the legal right-of-way agreement is on file at your county courthouse. It spells out what the operator may do in the easement and what restrictions apply to you as the landowner.

911

Suspect a pipeline leak?

Leave the area on foot immediately and warn others. From a safe distance, call 911 and the pipeline company on the marker. Don’t use phones, switches, vehicles or anything that could spark near the leak.
