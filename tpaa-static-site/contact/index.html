Who to call depends on the situation. Here's the quick guide.

Emergency or suspected leak

Leave the area first. Then call 911 and the pipeline company listed on the nearest marker, from a safe distance.

Before you dig

Dial 811 or visit texas811.org at least two working days before starting any digging project to have pipelines and utility lines located and marked. It's free and it's the law.

Right-of-way questions

To report prohibited activity on a pipeline right-of-way, or for questions about right-of-way restrictions, call the company listed on the pipeline marker. If a right-of-way crosses your property, you can obtain a copy of the legal agreement at your county courthouse.

Gas piping at your home

Pipelines that connect gas appliances to distribution lines are owned and maintained by the property owner and should be periodically inspected. Some distribution companies provide this service; licensed plumbers are also authorized to do it. For non-emergency questions about the piping that connects to your appliances, contact a licensed plumber.

Reach TPAA

To request information about the Texas Pipeline Awareness Alliance or ask general questions, email info@pipeline-safety.org.

You can also find us on Facebook.

911

Suspect a pipeline leak?

Leave the area on foot immediately and warn others. From a safe distance, call 911 and the pipeline company on the marker. Don’t use phones, switches, vehicles or anything that could spark near the leak.
